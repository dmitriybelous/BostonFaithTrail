# Tour Stop: Boston Common
## A Field of Preaching & Revival
*Boston Christian Tour*

---

You're standing on one of the most historically significant pieces of ground in American Christianity. Boston Common has been many things over the centuries — a cattle grazing field, a military parade ground, a public gathering place. But for Christians, it is hallowed ground. Because the Gospel has been proclaimed here to some of the largest crowds ever assembled in America.

Let's walk through the preachers who stood right here.

---

## Jonathan Edwards — The Theologian Who Lit the Fuse
**1731 & the Great Awakening**

Before we talk about the crowds on this Common, we need to talk about the man whose theology fueled everything.

In 1731, Jonathan Edwards preached a landmark sermon to the Boston clergy titled *"God Glorified in the Work of Redemption."* In it he fired a warning shot at the growing idea that people could earn their own salvation through good works and moral living. Edwards thundered back to the Calvinist roots — God is sovereign, man is helpless, and salvation comes by grace alone through faith in Christ.

Edwards then unleashed a religious revival in Massachusetts that paved the way for the Great Awakening, the evangelical movement that swept through all of British North America in the 1740s.

Edwards never preached a massive outdoor sermon here on the Common, but his influence saturated every revival that happened in Boston. He was the theologian behind the Great Awakening — the man who gave the movement its doctrinal backbone. His most famous sermon, *"Sinners in the Hands of an Angry God,"* was so powerful that when he preached it in Enfield, Connecticut in 1741, the people shrieked and wept so loudly that Edwards was forced to stop preaching. The pastors went down among the people and prayed with them in groups.

Edwards never chased crowds. He spoke in a quiet, measured voice. But God moved through him like a fire.

---

## George Whitefield — The Grand Itinerant
**September & October 1740**

If Edwards was the theologian, George Whitefield was the spark.

In 1740, Whitefield arrived in Boston after touring New England. On September 20, he preached at First Church and then outside it to about 8,000 people who could not get in. The next day he preached outdoors to about 15,000 people. He returned to Boston on October 12 to preach to 30,000 people before continuing his tour.

His farewell sermon on Boston Common drew **23,000 people** — more than Boston's entire population. It was probably the largest crowd that had ever gathered in America up to that date.

Think about that. More people than lived in the entire city came out to hear one man preach the Gospel.

What made Whitefield so extraordinary? His preaching style was unlike anything the colonists had encountered. Where typical preachers droned in a monotone while reading from the Bible, Whitefield let his emotions pour through — passionately, urgently — and crowds would unleash their emotions to make an immediate, personal connection with God.

Whitefield believed that every person — regardless of class, wealth, education, or prestige — could freely choose Jesus Christ. His preaching centered on inner conversion and the need for the New Birth — to be *"born again."*

---

## The Revival Impact

Following Whitefield's departure in October 1740, the revival continued in Boston without interruption for a year and a half. The very face of the town was strangely altered — even the lowest and crudest individuals on the streets left their usual rudeness.

Some estimates conclude that in New England alone, **10% of the population — approximately 30,000 people** — were added to churches between 1740 and 1742.

A fun fact: About 80% of all American colonists heard Whitefield preach at least once. Other than royalty, he was perhaps the only living person whose name would have been recognized by any colonial American.

---

## Billy Graham — The 20th Century's Greatest Evangelist
**April 1950**

Two hundred and ten years after Whitefield packed Boston Common, another evangelist came — and this ground shook again.

In April 1950, Billy Graham waged a five-day campaign in Boston that began at Park Street Church, moved to Mechanics Hall, and ended with a service on Boston Common that drew a crowd of **50,000**. He held that gathering despite freezing temperatures. Fifty thousand people standing in the cold to hear the Gospel preached. The spirit of Whitefield's 1740 revival had returned — two centuries later, in a different world, but on the same ground.

Earlier that same January, 16,000 people had filled the Boston Garden — more than 5,000 were turned away — to hear Graham preach, in what the *Globe* called "the greatest revival meeting New England has seen in the present generation." An estimated 3,000 people streamed to the stage in response to Graham's call to accept Christ.

Graham returned to Boston multiple times throughout his ministry. By the time of his last crusade in 2005, he had preached 417 live crusades to more people in person than anyone in the history of Christianity.

---

## What This Ground Means

From the Puritan founders, to the Great Awakening, to Billy Graham — this Common has been a place where heaven touched earth. Where ordinary people heard the Gospel and were never the same.

The preachers changed. The centuries changed. The crowds came and went. But the message never changed:

> *"For God so loved the world that He gave His only begotten Son, that whosoever believeth in Him should not perish but have everlasting life."*
>
> — John 3:16

That is what was preached here. And it still stands.
