# Tour Stop: Mary Dyer

**Massachusetts State House, Beacon Hill, Boston**
*Boston Christian Tour*

---

Take a moment to look at this statue. Notice how calm she is — hands folded, expression peaceful. For someone who was executed just steps from where we're standing, that serenity is remarkable. And that's exactly the point.

This is Mary Dyer, and her story is one of faith, conviction, and the cost of following your conscience before God.

## A City Upon a Hill

Mary came to Boston in the 1630s as a Puritan, part of the great wave of English Christians who crossed the Atlantic to build what their leader John Winthrop famously called "a city upon a hill" — a phrase drawn straight from Jesus' Sermon on the Mount. They came seeking freedom to worship God as they believed Scripture called them to. The deep irony is that once they arrived, they allowed no one else that same freedom.

Boston's Puritan leaders demanded total religious conformity. Disagree with the established church, and you could be fined, whipped, or banished. Mary began to question this. She befriended Anne Hutchinson, the bold woman who dared to teach Scripture in her home — and was banished for it. Mary stood by her, and was exiled as well.

## The Inner Light

Years later, in England, Mary encountered the Quakers — a movement that believed every soul had direct access to God through what they called the "Inner Light" of Christ. No clergy required. No state church to answer to. Just a person, their Bible, and the Holy Spirit. For Mary, it resonated deeply.

But back in Massachusetts, Quakerism had been made a criminal offense. Quakers were imprisoned, brutally whipped, and ultimately the colony passed a law making it punishable by death. The very colony founded on religious freedom was now executing Christians for worshipping differently.

## A Calling from God

Mary knew the law. She came back anyway — not out of recklessness, but out of what she described as a calling from God to bear witness against a grave injustice.

She was arrested and sentenced to hang. In 1659 she was actually led to the gallows on Boston Common, a noose placed around her neck — only to be reprieved at the last moment in a cruel act designed to break her spirit. She was sent home to Rhode Island with a warning never to return.

She returned.

When the authorities demanded to know why she kept coming back, Mary wrote a letter to the General Court of Massachusetts that has survived to this day. In it she declared:

> *"My life is not accepted, neither availeth me, in comparison with the liberty of the Truth."*

She wasn't seeking martyrdom. She was seeking justice — and she believed God had placed that burden on her heart.

## The Final Sentence

When she stood before the court for the final time and was sentenced to death, she responded without flinching:

> *"The will of the Lord be done."*

The magistrate, perhaps unsettled, told her that she had brought her death upon herself. Mary answered with quiet clarity:

> *"Nay, I came to keep blood guiltiness from you, desiring you to repeal the unrighteous and unjust law made against the innocent servants of the Lord."*

Even in her final moments she was not pleading for her own life — she was pleading for the souls of the men who were about to take it.

## June 1, 1660

On June 1, 1660, Mary Dyer was led to the gallows on Boston Common. Before she climbed the ladder, onlookers called out, asking if she was not ashamed of what she was doing. She replied:

> *"Nay, I am not. This is to me the hour of greatest joy I ever had in this world."*

She was offered her life one final time if she would leave Massachusetts and not return. She refused. She believed God had called her to stand there, and she would not move.

Her death echoed the words of the apostles before her — men and women who also stood before governing authorities and said, *"We must obey God rather than men."* (Acts 5:29)

## A Witness That Changed the Law

Her sacrifice was not in vain. Within a year, King Charles II ordered Massachusetts to stop executing Quakers. Her witness had changed the law.

The sculptor Sylvia Shaw Judson — herself a Quaker — chose not to show Mary at the gallows. She shows her at rest, hands folded as if in prayer, face calm. It is the image of someone whose conscience is entirely at peace with God.

She sits here today outside the Massachusetts State House as a permanent witness — a reminder that religious freedom in America was not simply written into law. It was purchased, in part, by the faithfulness of a Christian woman who would not deny her convictions even at the cost of her life.
